Antinopolis

Roman Period

Erin Kurth

Introduction

The city of Antinopolis was founded by the Roman emperor Hadrian to honor the memory of Antinous, his former lover, who drowned in the Nile during 130CE. Antinopolis was the site of the cult of Antinous, whom Hadrian deified after his death, and who was likened to Osiris due to his manner of death. Architecturally, Antinopolis was constructed in the Greco-Roman style and contrasted sharply with the Egyptian architecture of surrounding cities. The city had its own Senate and Prytaneus (President), and became the capital of the nome of Thebaid under the reign of Diocletian. In the 4th century CE, Antinopolois became the seat of a Christian bishop, and is now listed by the Catholic Church as being a titular see. Antinopolis was abandoned at some point during the 10th century for unknown reasons, and today very little remains of this ancient city. Many of the older structures were removed to make room for sugar factories, however several temple ruins can still be found here, as well as some remains of a Roman Circus that once existed there.


Geography of Site

The Roman city of Antinoopolis was built on the ruins of an abandoned New Kingdom Egyptian site, located slightly south of the city of Besa. The city was established on a necropolis and the site of a temple of Ramses the II. As a center of trade, Antinoopolis was connected by the Via Hadriana--an ancient Roman road--to Berenice Troglodytica, an Egyptian port located on the Red Sea (Encyclopedia Britannica). This connection allowed for trade with India and China, and proved advantageous for the ancient city. Located in middle Egypt on the east bank of the Nile River, Antinoopolis, as well as its neighboring city of Hermopolis, was a part of a 15km-wide floodplain. (Bunbury, J.; Malouta, M., 2012) There is evidence that the Nile was slowly migrating towards the city of Antinoopolis at a rate of approximately 2.2 km/millenium until 130 CE and then stopped, which “is suggestive of an anchoring of the river by the Roman town by a mixture of revetment of the banks and reduction in the erosive power of the Nile by removal of water for irrigation.” (Bunbury, J.; Malouta, M., 2012) During a visit to the ancient city in 1798, French scientist Jomand describes Antinoopolis as having walls on three sides, with the exception being the side that faced the Nile River itself. (academic.org subsource, Description de l’Egypte) Other sources also describe the city as being “walled round.” (Richardson, R., 1822) Pertaining to the structure of the city itself, there is evidence of there having been two main streets, perpendicular to one another, each lined with columns and terminating in gates. (Richardson, R., 1822) The city had a trapezoidal shape and a grid-like plan, with streets being divided into quarters and blocks. On the east side of the city was a natural valley (also known as a wadi) that bordered a desert landscape.
